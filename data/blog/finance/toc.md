If you studied **finance** at university, you’d usually encounter a handful of **core pillars**. Capital markets is one of them—but it fits into a bigger map.

Here’s the clean, high-level structure most finance degrees revolve around, plus what you actually _learn_ in each.

---

## 1️⃣ Capital Markets ✅ (You already named this)

**How money flows through markets**

This is about public markets and financial instruments.

You’d study:

- Stocks, bonds, derivatives (options, futures)
- IPOs, secondary markets
- Market efficiency (EMH)
- Valuation basics (discounted cash flow, multiples)
- Risk/return, diversification
- Interest rates & yield curves

Careers tied to this:

- Investment banking
- Trading
- Asset management
- Equity research

---

## 2️⃣ Corporate Finance

**How companies make financial decisions**

This is internal, decision-making finance.

You’d study:

- Capital budgeting (NPV, IRR)
- Cost of capital (WACC)
- Capital structure (debt vs equity)
- Dividends & share buybacks
- Mergers & acquisitions
- Financial statement analysis

Core idea:

> “What projects should we invest in, and how do we fund them?”

Careers:

- Corporate finance / FP&A
- M&A
- Strategy roles
- CFO track

---

## 3️⃣ Investments & Portfolio Management

**How to allocate capital intelligently**

Related to capital markets, but more decision-focused.

You’d study:

- Portfolio theory (Markowitz)
- CAPM, beta
- Asset allocation
- Active vs passive investing
- Performance measurement
- Behavioral finance (why humans make bad money decisions)

Careers:

- Asset management
- Wealth management
- Hedge funds
- Financial advisory

---

## 4️⃣ Financial Institutions & Banking

**How the financial system functions**

This zooms out to the plumbing of finance.

You’d study:

- Commercial banking
- Investment banking
- Central banks & monetary policy
- Credit creation
- Regulation (Basel, capital requirements)
- Financial crises

Core question:

> “How does the system avoid collapsing… or fail to?”

Careers:

- Banking
- Risk and compliance
- Central banks
- Financial regulation

---

## 5️⃣ Risk Management

**Measuring and controlling uncertainty**

This is where finance meets probability.

You’d study:

- Credit risk
- Market risk
- Liquidity risk
- Derivatives as risk hedges
- Value at Risk (VaR)
- Stress testing

Careers:

- Risk analyst
- Quant roles
- Treasury
- Insurance

---

## 6️⃣ Financial Modeling & Valuation

**Turning theory into numbers**

Often embedded across courses, but crucial.

You’d learn:

- Excel / financial modeling
- Forecasting cash flows
- Company valuation
- Scenario & sensitivity analysis

This is the “hard skill” layer that employers care about.

---

## 7️⃣ Accounting (Foundational, not optional)

**The language finance is written in**

Even if your degree is “finance,” accounting is unavoidable.

You’d learn:

- Income statements, balance sheets, cash flow
- Accruals vs cash
- Revenue recognition
- Financial ratios
- Earnings quality

Finance without accounting comprehension = guessing.

---

## 8️⃣ Quantitative Methods & Econometrics

**Math + data for decision-making**

Depends on how technical your program is.

You’d study:

- Statistics
- Regression
- Time-series analysis
- Forecasting
- Optimization

More prominent if aiming for:

- Quant finance
- Trading
- Research roles

---

## 9️⃣ Behavioral Finance (Often later / elective)

**Why markets aren’t rational**

This is psychology applied to money.

You’d study:

- Biases (loss aversion, overconfidence)
- Herd behavior
- Bubbles and crashes
- Market anomalies

This explains _why_ real markets differ from textbook models.

---

## Big Picture Mental Model

You can think of finance like this:

- **Accounting** → records reality
- **Corporate finance** → makes business decisions
- **Capital markets** → allocate resources
- **Investments** → choose where money goes
- **Risk management** → prevents blowing up
- **Financial institutions** → keep the system running
