# 📐 Trigonometric Identities Hub

This hub organizes the core trigonometric identities used in calculus, algebraic simplification, and integration techniques.

# 🧠 Core Idea

Trigonometric identities are **equivalent expressions of the same relationships between trig functions**.

They are used to:

- simplify expressions
- transform integrals
- solve equations
- convert between trig forms

---

# 🔗 Pythagorean Identities

Fundamental relationships derived from the unit circle:

$$
\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1
$$

$$
1 + \tan^2 x = \sec^2 x
$$

$$
1 + \cot^2 x = \csc^2 x
$$

### Key Use Cases:

- simplifying squared trig expressions
- rewriting integrands in integration
- converting between trig functions

---

# 🔁 Angle Sum and Difference Formulas

Used to evaluate trig functions of combined angles:

$$
\sin(a \pm b) = \sin a \cos b \pm \cos a \sin b
$$

$$
\cos(a \pm b) = \cos a \cos b \mp \sin a \sin b
$$

$$
\tan(a \pm b) = \frac{\tan a \pm \tan b}{1 \mp \tan a \tan b}
$$

### Key Use Cases:

- exact value computations
- trigonometric simplification
- signal/oscillation problems

---

# 📊 Double Angle Formulas

Expressions involving \(2x\):

$$
\sin(2x) = 2\sin x \cos x
$$

$$
\cos(2x) = \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x
$$

Equivalent forms:

$$
\cos(2x) = 2\cos^2 x - 1
$$

$$
\cos(2x) = 1 - 2\sin^2 x
$$

### Key Use Cases:

- integration simplification
- rewriting powers of trig functions

---

# 📉 Half-Angle Formulas

Used to express squares of trig functions:

$$
\sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2}
$$

$$
\cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2}
$$

### Key Use Cases:

- reducing powers
- evaluating integrals of \(\sin^2 x\), \(\cos^2 x\)

---

# 🔄 Product-to-Sum Identities

Convert products into sums:

$$
\sin a \cos b = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(a+b) + \sin(a-b)]
$$

$$
\cos a \cos b = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(a+b) + \cos(a-b)]
$$

$$
\sin a \sin b = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(a-b) - \cos(a+b)]
$$

### Key Use Cases:

- integration of trig products
- signal decomposition

---

# 🔁 Sum-to-Product Identities

Convert sums into products:

Used when simplifying oscillatory expressions or factoring trig sums.

---

# 🧠 Strategy Insight

Trig identities are not isolated formulas — they are **transformation tools**.

They help you move between:

- products ↔ sums
- powers ↔ linear forms
- complex expressions ↔ integrable forms

---

# 📌 When to use trig identities

Look for:

- powers like \(\sin^2 x, \cos^2 x\)
- products like \(\sin x \cos x\)
- expressions involving \(a \pm b\)
- integrals involving trig expressions
