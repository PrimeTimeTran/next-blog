# Imaginary Numbers

## Core idea

Imaginary numbers extend the real number system to include solutions to equations like:

- $x^2 + 1 = 0$

This equation has no solution in real numbers because no real number squared equals $-1$.

To resolve this, we define a new unit:

- $i = \sqrt{-1}$

So:

- $i^2 = -1$

## What they are

An imaginary number is any real multiple of $i$:

- $bi$, where $b \in \mathbb{R}$

Examples:

- $3i$
- $-0.5i$
- $i$

## Complex numbers (context)

Imaginary numbers combine with real numbers to form complex numbers:

- $a + bi$

Where:

- $a$ = real part
- $bi$ = imaginary part

Examples:

- $2 + 3i$
- $-1 + i$
- $5$ (purely real, since $b = 0$)

## Key intuition

- Real numbers live on a number line.
- Imaginary numbers introduce a second dimension.
- Together, complex numbers form a plane (real + imaginary axes).

Think of:

- Real axis → left/right movement
- Imaginary axis → up/down movement

## Why they matter

Imaginary numbers are not “fake”—they’re essential in:

- Electrical engineering (AC circuits)
- Signal processing (Fourier transforms)
- Quantum mechanics
- Control systems
- Solving polynomial equations

## Basic arithmetic rules

- $i^2 = -1$
- $i^3 = -i$
- $i^4 = 1$
- Powers of $i$ repeat every 4 steps

## Example simplification

- $(2i)(3i) = 6i^2 = 6(-1) = -6$

## Summary

Imaginary numbers arise when we extend the real numbers to make all quadratic equations solvable. They are built from the unit $i = \sqrt{-1}$ and form the imaginary axis of the complex plane.
