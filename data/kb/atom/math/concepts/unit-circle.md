# 📌 Unit Circle

## 🧠 Core Idea

The unit circle defines trigonometric functions using a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin.

Instead of being about triangles, trig becomes:

> **Angles → coordinates on a circle**

---

## 🔵 Definition

A point on the unit circle is defined as:

Where:

- $x = \cos(\theta)$
- $y = \sin(\theta)$

---

## 📐 Key Relationship

$$ x = \cos(\theta), \quad y = \sin(\theta) $$

So every angle maps to a point:

> $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$

---

## 🔑 Key Insight

The unit circle generalizes trig:

- No longer just triangles
- Works for **all angles (including negatives and > 90°)**
- Defines trig as **circular motion**

---

## 🔄 Why it matters

It explains:

- Periodicity (repeating behavior of sin/cos)
- Negative angles (rotation direction)
- Trig beyond right triangles
- Waves and oscillations in physics

---

## 🧭 Geometric Interpretation

For an angle $\theta$:

- Rotate a point starting at (1, 0)
- Move counterclockwise by $\theta$
- The new coordinates give:
  - x → cosine
  - y → sine

---

## 📊 Key Anchor Values

- $0$ → (1, 0)
- $\\frac{\pi}{2}$ → (0, 1)
- $\pi$ → (-1, 0)
- $\\frac{3\pi}{2}$ → (0, -1)

---

## 🧠 One-line Memory Hook

> The unit circle turns trig into **rotation on a circle where coordinates _are_ sine and cosine**
