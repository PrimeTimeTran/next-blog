# 📘 Continuity in Limits

Continuity describes when a function behaves “smoothly” at a point, and limits are the tool used to define that smoothness precisely.

---

# 🧠 Core Idea

> A function is continuous if the limit matches the function’s actual value.

---

# 📊 Formal Condition

A function $f(x)$ is continuous at $x = a$ if:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)
$$

This requires all three conditions:

---

# 🧩 Continuity Requirements

1. $f(a)$ is defined
2. $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists
3. $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$

---

# 🔍 Meaning

Continuity means:

> no breaks, jumps, or holes at the point

The function’s behavior approaching $a$ matches its actual value at $a$.

---

# 📈 Limit Perspective

Continuity is fundamentally a **limit agreement condition**:

- limit describes approach behavior
- function value describes actual point value
- continuity ensures they match

---

# ⚠️ Common Discontinuities

A function fails continuity if:

- **Hole** → limit exists, but $f(a)$ is undefined
- **Jump** → left and right limits differ
- **Infinite discontinuity** → function blows up

---

# 🧠 Key Insight

> Continuity is not about drawing without lifting your pen — it is about limits agreeing with values.

---

# 🔗 Why it matters

Continuity is required for:

- Intermediate Value Theorem
- Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
- many limit simplifications
- derivative existence

---

# 🧭 Mental Model

> The limit describes where you are going; continuity checks if you actually arrive there.

---

# 📌 Summary

- Continuity means limit equals function value
- Must satisfy existence + equality conditions
- Ensures no breaks in behavior at a point
- Fundamental assumption in most of calculus
