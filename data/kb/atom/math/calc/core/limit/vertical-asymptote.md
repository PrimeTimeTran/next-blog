### 🧠 Vertical Asymptote

A **vertical asymptote** is a vertical line ( x = a ) where a function grows without bound as it approaches ( a ).

---

### 📌 Formal idea

A function ( f(x) ) has a vertical asymptote at ( x = a ) if:

$$
	\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \pm \infty \quad \text{or} \quad \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = \pm \infty
$$

---

### 🔍 Intuition

As $x$ gets closer and closer to $x$, the function values:

- shoot up to $+\infty$, or
- drop to $-\infty$

The graph “blows up” near that line.

---

### 📍 Common sources

Vertical asymptotes often happen when:

- A denominator becomes zero (rational functions)
- Logs hit zero: $\log(x)$
- Tangents become undefined: $\tan(x)$

---

### 📘 Example

$$
	f(x) = \frac{1}{x - 2}
$$

- Denominator is zero at $x = 2$
- So $x = 2$ is a vertical asymptote

---

### ⚠️ Key distinction

- Vertical asymptote ≠ hole  
   (a hole is a removable discontinuity; an asymptote is infinite behavior)
