### 🧠 Infinite Limits

An **infinite limit** describes a situation where a function grows without bound (positive or negative) as ( x ) approaches a value.

---

## 📌 Formal definition

A function $f(x)$ has an infinite limit at $x = a$ if:

$$
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty
\quad \text{or} \quad
\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = -\infty
$$

This means:

- As $x \to a$, $f(x)$ increases or decreases without bound.

---

## 📍 One-sided versions matter

Infinite limits are often direction-dependent:

$$
\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \pm \infty
\quad \text{or} \quad
\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = \pm \infty
$$

Both sides do **not need to match**.

---

## 🔥 Intuition

As you get closer to $x = a$:

- the function values become extremely large (positive or negative)
- they do not settle toward a finite number
- the graph “blows up”

---

## 📊 Example

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{x - 2}
$$

As $x \to 2$:

- $x \to 2^+ \Rightarrow f(x) \to +\infty$
- $x \to 2^- \Rightarrow f(x) \to -\infty$

So:

- Infinite limits exist at $x = 2$
- This also creates a **vertical asymptote**

---

## 🚨 Key idea

Infinite limits are **not numbers**

They describe:

> unbounded behavior, not a finite output

So:

$$
\infty \notin \mathbb{R}
$$

---

## 🔗 Connection to other concepts

- Vertical asymptotes = geometric manifestation of infinite limits
- Indeterminate forms often _lead into_ infinite limits
- Rational functions are the most common source

---

## ⚠️ Common mistake

Confusing:

- “undefined” (hole or missing value)  
   with
- “infinite behavior” (blows up to ±∞)

Only the second one is an infinite limit.
