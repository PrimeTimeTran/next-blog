# Calc 3 (Multivariable Calculus)

Calc 3 extends single-variable calculus to functions with **multiple inputs** and **vector-valued quantities**, focusing on how change behaves in higher dimensions.

---

## **Core Objects**

### 1. Multivariable functions

$$
f(x, y), \quad f(x, y, z)
$$

- Output depends on more than one variable
- Represents surfaces instead of curves

---

### 2. Vectors and geometry

- Points in space: $(x, y, z)$
- Vectors: magnitude + direction
- Used to describe motion, forces, and fields

---

## **Derivatives in higher dimensions**

### 3. Partial derivatives

$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$

- Differentiate with respect to one variable at a time
- Treat others as constants

---

### 4. Gradient (direction of steepest increase)

$\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right)$

- Points uphill fastest
- Generalizes derivative to multiple dimensions

---

### 5. Directional derivative

- Rate of change in any direction, not just axes
- Depends on both gradient and direction vector

---

## **Integrals in higher dimensions**

### 6. Double and triple integrals

- Accumulate values over areas and volumes

$\iint f(x,y),dA$
$\iiint f(x,y,z),dV$

- Compute mass, volume, probability, etc.

---

## **Vector calculus (core Calc 3 layer)**

### 7. Line integrals

- Integration along curves

### 8. Surface integrals

- Integration over surfaces in 3D space

---

## **Big theorems (the “connectors”)**

- **Green’s Theorem** → relates line integrals ↔ area integrals
- **Stokes’ Theorem** → relates surface integrals ↔ boundary curves
- **Divergence Theorem** → relates flux through surfaces ↔ volume behavior

---

## **Big picture intuition**

Calc 3 is about:

> Moving from “change along a line” → to “change in space”

- Derivatives become **gradients (vectors)**
- Integrals become **area/volume accumulation**
- Theorems connect local behavior ↔ global structure

---

## **One-line summary**

Calc 3 = calculus of **functions in space**, where derivatives become vectors and integrals measure accumulation over areas, volumes, and surfaces.
