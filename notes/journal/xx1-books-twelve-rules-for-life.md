# 12 Rules for Life

An Antidote to Chaos by Dr. Jordan Peterson provides actionable advice on taking personal responsibility, imposing order on chaos, and
finding meaning through suffering. The rules emphasize individual accountability, truth-telling, self-care, and developing resilience to improve oneself and society.

## The 12 Rules

1. Stand up straight with your shoulders back: Adopt a posture of confidence and competence to change your mental state.

2. Treat yourself like someone you are responsible for helping: Care for yourself with the same kindness you offer others.

3. Make friends with people who want the best for you: Surround yourself with supportive people, not those who enable bad habits.

4. Compare yourself to who you were yesterday, not who someone else is today: Focus on personal, incremental progress rather than social comparison.

5. Do not let your children do anything that makes you dislike them: Discipline children to ensure they are well-adjusted and likable.

6. Set your house in perfect order before you criticize the world: Take personal responsibility for your own life before blaming others.

7. Pursue what is meaningful (not what is expedient): Sacrifice instant gratification for long-term purpose.

8. Tell the truth—or, at least, don't lie: Lies corrupt, while truth brings stability and integrity.

9. Assume that the person you are listening to might know something you don't: Approach conversations with humility and a desire to learn.

10. Be precise in your speech: Define problems clearly to make them manageable.

11. Don't bother children when they are skateboarding: Allow people to take risks to develop competence and resilience.

12. Pet a cat when you encounter one on the street: Appreciate small moments of joy and beauty even in times of intense suffering.

## Key Themes

• Order vs. Chaos: The book balances the structure of order with the unpredictability of chaos.
• Meaning over Happiness: Happiness is fleeting; searching for meaning offers a more robust framework for living.
• Responsibility: The core idea is that personal, intentional responsibility reduces suffering and brings meaning.
