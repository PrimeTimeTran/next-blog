## The Feynman Technique is a four-step mental model for learning or reviewing any subject by explaining it in simple, intuitive language as if teaching a child. Developed by Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman, it identifies knowledge gaps by forcing the learner to simplify complex jargon, ultimately strengthening comprehension and long-term recall.

The Four Steps of the Feynman Technique

1. Choose a Concept and Study It: Write down the topic at the top of a blank sheet of paper and review material until you can explain it.
2. Teach it to a Child (Simplify): Explain the concept in your own words, using simple, plain language. Avoid technical jargon and focus on analogies.
3. Identify Gaps and Review: When you get stuck or find yourself using complicated language, go back to the source material to strengthen your understanding.
4. Organize and Refine: Create a final, coherent explanation. Review it, and if possible, read it aloud to ensure it flows logically.

Key Benefits

• Faster Learning: Focuses on understanding core principles rather than memorization.
• Identifies Gaps: Explicitly highlights what you do not know, preventing overconfidence.
• Improved Retention: Simplification and active recall strengthen mental connections.

Examples in Action

• Physics: Instead of saying "atoms jiggle," explaining heat as "atoms bumping into each other to pass energy".
• Biology: Explaining mitosis not just through the phases (prophase, etc.), but as the process where "one cell acts like a rubber band to pull its genetic material into two identical, separate cells". [14]

Tips for Effectiveness

• Use Analogies: Create metaphors that relate the topic to everyday life.
• Use Visuals: Draw diagrams to illustrate complex processes.
• Go Deep: Continue refining the explanation until you can explain the "why" behind the topic.
